The Large Interferometer for Exoplanets (LIFE) mission concept aims to detect
and characterize terrestrial exoplanets in the mid-infrared through space-based nulling
interferometry. As the mission remains in the simulation and design study phase, forecasting its
potential scientific return requires robust modeling of instrument performance and astrophysical
populations. This internship project establishes the theoretical and computational background
needed to estimate the exoplanet detection yield of LIFE under varying assumptions. The
work involves a structured review of mission concept papers, interferometric principles, and prior
exoplanet yield studies; hands-on engagement with existing LIFE simulation notebooks; and the
execution of Monte Carlo frameworks that generate synthetic planetary systems and evaluate
their detectability. Parameter-space exploration will assess the influence of baseline length,
wavelength coverage, detection thresholds, and astrophysical priors on predicted yields.
